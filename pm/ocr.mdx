# LargeFileBridge — OCR (read the text out of images & videos)

`LargeFileBridge` can **read the text that is visible in the pixels** of an **image** or a **video** and
store it so those words become **searchable, greppable, and re-findable without opening the file**. A
screenshot of a terminal, a slide deck exported to MP4, a photo of a whiteboard, a receipt, a news chyron,
a street sign, burned-in subtitles — all of it is text the user can *see* but that no `grep` can reach,
because it is pixels, not characters. OCR turns those pixels back into characters.

This is the **third transaction type**, and the reason it exists as its own transaction rather than a corner
of an existing one is that it answers a **different question** from either sibling:

| Transaction | The question it answers | Input kinds | Output |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Transcription** ([Transcribe.mdx](./Transcribe.mdx)) | *"What was **said**?"* | audio + video | Speech → text, verbatim |
| **AI description** ([ai_description.mdx](./ai_description.mdx)) | *"What is **seen**?"* | image + video | A vision model's **prose** about the frame |
| **OCR** (this spec) | *"What does it **say on screen**?"* | image + video + **PDF** | The **literal glyphs**, verbatim, with positions |

OCR has the **widest** input axis of the three, and PDF is the reason (§1.7.1). A document is a stack of pages,
each of which is just pixels once rendered — so a scanned contract, a signed agreement, or a slide export all
become searchable text the same way a screenshot does. This is where OCR stops being the mirror of AI
description (image + video) and becomes a superset: **image + video + PDF.**

Implemented in code: `ocrKindFor()` in `~/BGit/Bryan_git/LargeFileBridge/code/packages/backend/src/modules/ocr/ocr.service.ts` returns `image` / `video` / `pdf` (else null); the PDF page-render path is `renderPdfPages()` in `~/BGit/Bryan_git/LargeFileBridge/code/packages/backend/src/modules/ocr/pdf.ts`.

The distinction is not academic. AI description **paraphrases** and may *mention* that a sign exists; OCR
**quotes** the sign, exactly, and can be searched for. A slide deck video described by Gemini yields "a
slide listing quarterly figures"; the same video OCR'd yields the actual figures. They compose — they do not
substitute.

Two things make this feature worth pinning down: **the two-speed rule** (§2 — image = slow/accurate, video =
fast at a 15-second stride, which is the whole performance story; a **PDF page reads accurate**, like an
image, because a page is a document and there are far fewer pages than a video has frames), and **the engine
being a library rather than a `brew` tool** (§3 — the explicit product directive, and the thing that keeps
OCR installable, offline, and charter-clean). One deliberate exception rides alongside the engine directive:
**PDF and video both lean on an external command** (poppler's `pdftoppm` to rasterize pages; ffmpeg to sample
frames) — the recognizer is still a library, but a page/frame has to be produced first, and that is a
subprocess we detect on PATH and state the absence of honestly (§6).

Siblings this builds on and does not repeat:

* [Transcribe.mdx](./Transcribe.mdx) — the **template** for every media-analysis surface: the entry points at
  every scale (§2), the tracking-area artifact placement (§3), the readiness gate, the calc engine. OCR

* [ai_description.mdx](./ai_description.mdx) — the **closest** sibling (same input kinds: image + video). Its

* [parallelization.mdx](./parallelization.mdx) — the **owner** of the Core Budget. OCR is a **local-CPU**
  consumer and draws the **mass-compute** budget, which is the deliberate asymmetry against describe (§10).
* [dialogs.mdx §5](./dialogs.mdx) — the **owner** of the unified batch-confirm popup. Every OCR producer

## 0. The symmetry contract is now a TRIPLE, not a pair (LOCKED)

[ai_description.mdx §0](./ai_description.mdx) locks a hard design rule: *everything transcription has, AI
description mirrors* — a user who has learned one has learned the other, and a developer touching one is
obliged to touch the other. **OCR joins that contract as the third column.** Adding a surface to any one of
the three now obliges adding it to all three.

| `.transcription` artifact | `.ai_description` artifact | **`.ocr` artifact** — §5 |

**The two deliberate asymmetries** — and they are the whole personality of this feature:

1. **OCR is LOCAL.** Transcription is local (an ASR model); AI description is the app's *one* deliberate
   network upload; **OCR is local like transcription and, unlike description, needs no key, no provider
   matrix, and no credentials popup** (§4). The whole `ai_credentials.mdx` axis is simply **absent** here —
   there is nothing to configure and nothing to leak.
2. **OCR is TWO-SPEED, keyed on media kind** (§2). Neither sibling has this: transcription runs one engine at
   one quality, description sends one prompt per kind. OCR deliberately runs a **different recognition
   level** for an image than for a video frame, because the workloads differ by three orders of magnitude.

The symmetry is asserted in code by the parallel module trees `modules/transcribe/` ↔ `modules/describe/` ↔
**`modules/ocr/`**, `lib/transcribe.ts` ↔ `lib/describe.ts` ↔ **`lib/ocr.ts`**, `TranscribeStatusIcon.tsx` ↔
`DescribeStatusIcon.tsx` ↔ **`OcrStatusIcon.tsx`**, and the paired fields on
`~/BGit/Bryan_git/LargeFileBridge/code/packages/shared/src/types.ts` (`TaskMetrics`, `FileRow`,
`OcrResult`/`OcrBatchResult` mirroring the describe/transcribe pairs).

## 1. Use cases

The feature earns its place through concrete, recurring jobs on the product owner's own tree. Each of these
is a case where the words are **visible but unsearchable** today.

### 1.1 "Which screenshot had that error message?"

`~/screenshots/` holds hundreds of PNGs. The user remembers a stack trace mentioning `ECONNREFUSED 8787`
but not which image it was in. Today: open them one by one. With OCR: every screenshot carries a `.ocr`
artifact, and one search across the tree lands on the file. This is the **highest-frequency** case, and it is
pure **image / slow-accurate** work (§2.1, §16).

### 1.2 "Find the slide that showed the pricing tier"

A 40-minute recorded deck (`.mp4`). The words live on the slides, not in the narration — so the
**transcript can't find them** (the presenter said "as you can see here"). OCR at a 15-second stride catches
every slide (a slide is on screen far longer than 15s) and timecodes it, so the user gets *"03:15 — Creator
$29/mo…"* and can seek straight there. This is the case the **15-second stride was chosen for** (§2.2.1).

### 1.3 The investigation tree — chyrons, exhibits, and documents-as-images

The Charlie Kirk / Intel / UAP investigation repos hold thousands of clips and screenshots whose evidentiary
content is **on-screen text**: a news lower-third naming a person, a court exhibit photographed rather than
scanned, a document screenshotted from a PDF. OCR makes that corpus **greppable**, which is the difference
between an archive and a research tool. It composes with the sibling artifacts: the `.transcription` gives
what was said, the `.ocr` gives what the chyron claimed, and the `.ai_description` gives the scene.

### 1.4 Receipts, statements, and the Medical LLC folder

Photographed receipts and statements are images of text. OCR gives a searchable text layer beside each one
without any upload — which matters, because these are **financial documents** and the charter's local-only
posture (§4) is the reason this use case is permissible at all. AI-describing a receipt would upload it to a
provider; **OCR never does**.

### 1.5 Burned-in subtitles on a downloaded clip

A clip with hardcoded subtitles and no audio track (or non-English audio). Transcription yields nothing
useful; OCR at the 15-second stride recovers the subtitle text. The **dedupe rule** (§2.2.3) matters here —
a subtitle persists across many frames and must not be recorded a dozen times.

### 1.6 "Is this file worth keeping?"

The rollup case. Before compressing or pinning a directory of 300 clips, the user wants to know **what is in
them**. OCR is the cheapest of the three transactions (no network, no model download in the primary path),
so it is often the **first** one run over a new tree — it costs nothing but CPU and answers "what is this?"
for anything screen-recorded.

### 1.7.1 The third kind — PDF (a stack of pages we rasterize)

A PDF is not media (no player, no IPFS payload) but it is a **first-class OCR target**, and for the common
case it is the **most valuable** one: legal contracts, signed agreements, invoices, and scanned documents are
overwhelmingly PDFs, and "read the text out of these PDFs" is exactly what a user in a folder of them wants.

* **How it works.** A PDF is a stack of **pages**. Each page is rendered to a PNG (poppler's `pdftoppm`, at
  150 DPI — crisp enough for body text without paying 4× the pixels of a 300-DPI render), and each page image
  is read at the **accurate** level — the same pass a standalone image gets (§2.1), because a page IS a
  document and accuracy is the whole point. The per-page texts are concatenated (blank-line separated) into
  the searchable `text`, and every block is tagged with its **1-based page** so a hit can name its page (§5.1).
* **Bounded + honest.** `max_pages` (default **500**) caps a pathological document exactly as `max_frames`
  caps a pathological video; when it bites, the artifact records `truncated: true` and the UI **says** so
  (§15.2 rule 7). The artifact also records `page_count` (from `pdfinfo`) and `pages_read`.
* **Concurrency.** A 200-page PDF is itself a batch; its page recognitions draw the **one shared recognition
  limiter** (§10.2), never a fresh per-document budget — the same rule the video frame path obeys.
* **The tool.** Rasterization needs poppler's `pdftoppm` on PATH — the **same posture** as the video path's
  ffmpeg (§6). Without it, every image still OCRs fine and every PDF reports `needs_pdf_tools` with a
  `brew install poppler` hint. Empty-text is still a SUCCESS (a blank page contributes nothing, §2.3).

Implemented in code: `ocrPdf()` and the `needs_pdf_tools` gate in
`~/BGit/Bryan_git/LargeFileBridge/code/packages/backend/src/modules/ocr/ocr.service.ts`; page rendering +
`pdfToolsPresent()` / `pdfPageCount()` in
`~/BGit/Bryan_git/LargeFileBridge/code/packages/backend/src/modules/ocr/pdf.ts`.

### 1.7 The negative case — what OCR is NOT for

* **Not audio.** An `.mp3` has no pixels. The OCR tab does not appear for audio (§7) and audio files are
  never candidates (§14).
* **Not other documents.** Only **PDF** is the document OCR kind today (§1.7.1). `.docx` / `.pptx` / `.txt`
  are not rasterized — a `.txt` already IS text, and Office formats are out of scope until asked for.
* **Not a description.** OCR on a photo of a beach returns nothing (there is no text) — and that is a
  **correct, successful** result, not a failure (§2.3). A file with `text: ""` is **OCR'd**; it simply has no
  text. It must never be re-offered as "not yet OCR'd" (§12.5).
* **Not a reporting service.** Like the perceptual-fingerprint feature, OCR reads the user's own files
  locally and tells **only the user** (§4).

## 2. The two-speed rule (LOCKED — the core of this spec)

**The recognition speed is keyed on the media kind, and it is not negotiable:**

| Kind | Pass | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Image** | **SLOW / ACCURATE** — `.accurate` recognition level, language correction **on** | One file, one pass. The user is asking about *this* image; accuracy is worth every millisecond. A 4K screenshot costs a few hundred ms even at the slow setting — there is nothing to save. |
| **Video** | **FAST** — `.fast` recognition level, language correction **off**, one frame every **15 s** | A 40-minute video at a 15s stride is **160 frames**. At the slow level that is minutes per file; at the fast level it is seconds. Multiply by a 2,000-file tree and the choice is the difference between a feature and an unusable one. |

This mapping is **exactly** what Apple's Vision framework exposes as `VNRequestTextRecognitionLevel.accurate`
vs `.fast` (§3.2), which is a large part of why that engine was chosen: our product rule and the engine's
native knob are the **same knob**.

### 2.1 The image path — slow, accurate, whole-file

An image is decoded once and handed to the engine at the **accurate** level with **language correction on**
(the engine may consult a lexicon to fix `rn` → `m` and similar confusions). No striding, no sampling, no
frame extraction, no ffmpeg. The result is a **single** text block plus per-observation bounding boxes and
confidences (§5.1). Detail: §16.

### 2.2 The video path — advance 15 seconds, grab a frame, fast-OCR it

A video is **sampled**, never fully decoded frame-by-frame. The pipeline:

1. **Probe** the clip for duration (`ffprobe`).
2. **Extract** one frame every **15 seconds** in **one** ffmpeg pass — `-vf fps=1/15` — writing numbered
   JPEGs into a **temporary** directory under the state dir. One pass, not one `-ss` seek per frame: a
   single sequential decode is dramatically cheaper than N seeks, and the frames come out already strided.
3. **Fast-OCR each frame**, in parallel (§10.2), tagging each result with its **timecode** (frame index × 15s).
4. **Dedupe** consecutive identical text (§2.2.3).
5. **Delete the temp frames** in a `finally`. **The user's original video is never modified or moved** —
   this is a read-only sampling pass. (Same posture as describe's compress-to-fit temp copy,
   [ai_description.mdx §3.3](./ai_description.mdx).)

Detail and the throughput numbers: §15.

#### 2.2.1 Why 15 seconds (LOCKED)

The stride is the product decision, and it is chosen against **how long on-screen text stays on screen**:

* A **slide** lives 30 s–5 min. A **chyron** lives 10–30 s. A **title card** lives 3–8 s. A **subtitle** lives
  2–5 s.
* At 15 s we catch **every slide and effectively every chyron** — the §1.2 and §1.3 cases, which are the ones
  the feature exists for — while sampling **0.07%** of a 30fps stream. Halving it to 7.5 s **doubles the cost**
  to buy mostly title cards; going to 30 s starts dropping chyrons.
* We accept, explicitly, that a **fast-moving subtitle track may be sampled rather than captured whole**
  (§1.5). The user who needs every subtitle wants the `subtitle_stride` override, not a different default.

The stride is a **setting** (`ocr.video_stride_seconds`, default **15**) so a user with a subtitle-heavy tree
can tighten it (§17), but **15 is the default and the number every UI states**.

#### 2.2.2 The first frame is never frame 0 (LOCKED)

`fps=1/15` emits its first frame at **t=0**, which on a huge share of real videos is **black, a fade-in, or a
platform splash** — i.e. reliably the **worst** frame in the clip. The extraction therefore offsets the
sampling grid by **half a stride** (`-ss 7.5` equivalent, via the filter's phase), so frames land at
**7.5 s, 22.5 s, 37.5 s…** — the **middle** of each 15-second window, where the content of that window
actually is. The count of frames is unchanged; only the phase moves.

**A clip shorter than one stride has exactly ONE window — itself.** The half-stride phase must therefore be
**clamped to the clip's own midpoint** (`duration/2`) and the `fps` grid dropped for a single frame. Applying
the fixed `-ss 7.5` to a sub-stride clip seeks at or past its end, **zero frames** reach the encoder, and
ffmpeg fails opening mjpeg at EOF with `-22 (Invalid argument)` — a message that names neither the length nor
the cause. This is not hypothetical: it failed **36 of 1,779** files in the 2026-07-16 batch — every video
under 15 s in the tree, and *only* those. The rule above is unchanged for anything ≥ one stride; this is the
same intent ("sample the middle of the window, never t=0") applied honestly to a clip that has one window.

Phases the grid, clamps sub-stride clips, and rejects a zero-frame extraction rather than laundering it into
an empty-but-`done` artifact, in code:
`~/BGit/Bryan_git/LargeFileBridge/code/packages/backend/src/modules/ocr/frames.ts` → `extractFrames()`.

#### 2.2.3 Consecutive-duplicate frames collapse (LOCKED)

A slide on screen for 3 minutes produces **12 identical frames** → 12 identical text blocks. Recording all 12
would bloat the artifact, wreck the timecode index, and make the text tab unreadable. So consecutive
observations whose **normalized text is identical** (whitespace-collapsed, case-sensitive) **collapse into one
entry with a time RANGE**:

```yaml
- start: 180.0
  end: 375.0        # the same slide across 13 samples → ONE entry, 3m15s wide
  text: "Q3 Revenue — $4.2M …"
```

Non-adjacent repeats do **not** collapse (a slide returned to later is a genuinely separate appearance, and
the user searching for it wants both timecodes). The comparison is on **normalized** text so that a
one-character OCR wobble between two samples of the same static slide does not defeat the collapse — a
**similarity threshold** (≥95% by Levenshtein ratio) is used rather than strict equality, because two
fast-level passes over the *same* pixels can and do differ by a character.

### 2.3 Empty is a SUCCESS, not a failure (LOCKED)

Most images have **no text**. A beach photo OCRs to `""`. This is the **correct** and **successful** outcome
and it must be recorded as one: `status: done`, `text: ""`, `blocks: []`. Getting this wrong is the bug that
would make the feature unusable at scale, because:

* If empty read back as "no artifact yet", **every text-free file in the tree would be re-offered forever** —
  the popup would say "2,000 files can be OCR'd" the morning after OCR'ing 2,000 files (the exact class of
  defect [ai_description.mdx §12.5](./ai_description.mdx) locks closed for descriptions).
* If empty read back as **`failed`**, the Processing page would show a wall of red for a run in which nothing
  went wrong.

So: **the artifact's existence with `status: done` is the truth**, and `text` may legitimately be empty.
Only a real fault (engine crash, unreadable file, ffmpeg missing on a video) is `failed`. The presence
indicator (§5.2) and the `ocred` metric (§12) both key on the **artifact**, never on the text being non-empty.

## 3. The engine — a LIBRARY, not a `brew install` (LOCKED, with the research)

**The product directive is explicit:** *"We actually want to have it where it's a library our web app uses,
not a `brew install` tool."* This is a real constraint with real consequences, and it is what §3.1's research
was conducted against.

**Why it matters.** Transcription's engines (`qwen3-asr` via pipx, `whisper` via brew) taught us the cost of
a **provisioned tool**: a consent dialog, a `withModelReady` gate, a tools-preflight page, a "Re-check"
button, a detection routine that must never report an installed tool missing
([Transcribe.mdx §5.2.1](./Transcribe.mdx)), and a support surface where a user's broken PATH becomes our
bug. **A library has none of that.** It arrives with `pnpm install`, it is version-pinned in the lockfile, it
cannot be half-installed, and it works on a fresh clone with zero user action. For a feature we want run over
**every image in the tree**, that difference decides adoption.

### 3.2 The decision (LOCKED) — Vision primary, Tesseract fallback

**Primary: `mac-ocr` / Apple Vision.** Every developer machine and every deployment target of this app today
is a Mac (the product owner's tower, the laptop, the Mac Studio "CLAW" server). Vision is **state-of-the-art
accuracy, free, on-device, requires no model download, is maintained by the OS vendor, and is
hardware-accelerated on Apple Silicon** — and, decisively, its **`.fast` / `.accurate` recognition levels are
precisely the two speeds §2 requires**. We do not have to build a speed abstraction; we have to *expose* one.

* **Image → accurate** (`usesLanguageCorrection` on) · **Video frame → `--fast`** (correction off).
* Results carry **bounding boxes + confidence** per observation, which is what fills `blocks[]` (§5.1).
* Languages are BCP-47 (`-l en-US -l ja-JP`), queryable per macOS version; the default follows the app locale
  and is overridable (§17).

**Fallback: `tesseract.js`.** Keeps the feature alive on Linux/Windows (the charter's *"server-side later"*
posture) and on a Mac where the Vision binary won't run. Selection is **automatic**, mirroring transcription's
qwen→whisper auto-fallback ([transcribe_engine.mdx](./transcribe_engine.mdx)); the resolved engine is
**recorded in the artifact** (§5.1) so a file OCR'd by the weaker engine can be identified and re-run later.

**The engine set is a pluggable adapter table**, exactly like describe's provider adapters — so adding
RapidOCR later is a new row, not a refactor.

The engine adapters, per-kind speed selection, availability probe, and auto-fallback are handled in code:
`~/BGit/Bryan_git/LargeFileBridge/code/packages/backend/src/modules/ocr/engines.ts` — `ENGINES` (`vision` / `tesseract`), `selectEngine(kind, requested)`, `engineStatus()`, `recognize(imagePath, { level })`.

### 3.3 The one provisioning caveat — Tesseract's language data must be VENDORED (LOCKED)

`mac-ocr` needs **nothing**: the binary ships in the package and Vision's models are part of macOS. That is
the whole point.

**`tesseract.js` is the exception, and it is a charter problem if ignored.** Out of the box it **fetches
`<lang>.traineddata` from a CDN on first use** — meaning the *fallback* engine would make LFBridge **phone
home**, silently, on a feature whose entire premise is that it does not (§4). Therefore:

* The language data for the **default language set** is **vendored into the package** and `langPath` /
  `corePath` are pointed at those **local files** (the `tesseract.js-offline` pattern). The fallback engine
  **must work with the network unplugged**, and that is an acceptance criterion (§18.4).
* If a user requests a language whose data is **not vendored**, that is a **permissioned provisioning
  download** — it reuses transcription's consent flow (`withModelReady` / the model-provision service,
  [transcribe_engine.mdx §3](./transcribe_engine.mdx)), surfacing a **consent dialog** that names the download.
  It is **never** silent, and never a side effect of clicking "OCR…".

Vendoring the traineddata and pinning `langPath`/`corePath` to local paths (so the fallback engine never reaches the network) are handled in code:
`~/BGit/Bryan_git/LargeFileBridge/code/packages/backend/src/modules/ocr/engines.ts` — the `tesseract` adapter's `createWorker(lang, oem, { langPath: VENDORED_LANG_DIR, corePath: VENDORED_CORE_DIR, gzip: false })`; data files under `~/BGit/Bryan_git/LargeFileBridge/code/packages/backend/assets/tessdata/`.

## 4. The charter posture — OCR NEVER uploads (LOCKED)

[ai_description.mdx §7](./ai_description.mdx) carries a prominent warning: describing a file is *"the ONE
deliberate external-network path in LFBridge."* **OCR is not a second one, and must never become one.**

* **No bytes leave the machine.** Vision recognizes on-device; the vendored Tesseract runs in-process on WASM
  (§3.3). There is **no** provider, **no** endpoint, **no** API key, **no** `ai_credentials` surface, and
  **no** credentials popup anywhere in this feature.
* **Therefore OCR needs no "explicit user action for privacy reasons."** It still only runs when clicked
  (menus.mdx §6.1's never-act-without-a-click guardrail is universal), but the *reason* is different: describe
  is click-gated because it **uploads**; OCR is click-gated because it **costs CPU**. This distinction is why
  OCR is a candidate for the To-Do batch flow with a far lower cost/safety bar than describe
  ([describe_calc_engine.mdx §5](./describe_calc_engine.mdx) vs §14.3).
* **It aligns with the perceptual-fingerprint rule.** The charter demands the fingerprint feature *"run
  entirely locally… must NOT phone home… we are not a reporting service."* OCR sits on **that** side of the
  line, not description's. A user's receipts (§1.4) and investigation exhibits (§1.3) are OCR'd with **no
  disclosure of any kind to anyone**.
* **Any change that introduces a network call to this module is a charter violation** and must be caught in
  review. This spec is the place that says so.

## 5. Where the OCR text lands — `.ocr`, appended, in the owning root's tracking area (LOCKED)

**Identical to `.transcription` and `.ai_description` in every respect but the extension.** The artifact
lives **inside the owning root's own committed tracking area**, in a **path-mirrored hierarchy**, under the
media's **full filename (extension kept)** with **`.ocr` appended** as a second extension.

> **Which tracking area — the storage-KIND rule** (owned by
> [artifact_placement_policy.mdx §0](./artifact_placement_policy.mdx)): `.lfbridge/` exists **only inside a
> working git repo**, where LFB is a guest. A **dedicated LFB file repo** — a Personal / Company / Community
> **SDL** — has **no `.lfbridge/` at all**; its root **is** the tracking area.
>
> | Storage kind | Example root | The OCR text lands at |
> | --- | --- | --- |
> | **`repo`** — a working git repo | `~/BGit/Bryan_git/charlie-kirk/` | `<root>/.lfbridge/videos/x.mp4.ocr` |
> | **`personal` / `company` / `community`** — an SDL | `~/BGit/Bryan_git/personal_large_files_bridge/` | `<root>/_Mirror/…/x.mp4.ocr` (**no** `.lfbridge/`) |

```
WORKING GIT REPO                          SDL FILE REPO (root IS the tracking area)
<repo>/                                   ~/BGit/Bryan_git/personal_large_files_bridge/
├── decks/                                └── _Mirror/
│   └── Q3.mp4       ← untouched              └── Politics/
└── .lfbridge/                                    └── x.mp4.ocr    ← NO .lfbridge/ segment
    └── decks/
        └── Q3.mp4.ocr   ← .ocr APPENDED
```

The `.ocr` token is a single constant — **`OCR_EXT`** — **appended**, never substituting the original
extension, so `Q3.mp4.ocr` and `Q3.mov.ocr` are **distinct** files (the same append-not-replace fix
[Transcribe.mdx §3.2](./Transcribe.mdx) locks). The placement **root** resolves through the **same shared
ordered rule** as its two siblings ([Transcribe.mdx §3.4](./Transcribe.mdx), authoritative) — walk up to the
nearest ancestor carrying `storage.yaml` / `.lfbridge/` / `.git/`, else the owning storage's dedicated repo,
else the storage root, else the first-time setup wizard, else a last-resort `.lfbridge/` in the media's own
directory — and classifies the root by **storage kind first**, since every SDL also carries a `.git/`. A tool
computing the path does `join(trackingBaseDir(root), relDir, mediaFilename + ".ocr")` and **never** hard-codes
`".lfbridge"` into the join.

**Why the tracking area:** it is **committed and travels with the repo**, so OCR text generated on the tower
is **reused on the laptop** with no re-run — and OCR text is small UTF-8, so committing it is cheap and
desirable. **No `*.ocr` git-ignore line is ever injected** (the same retirement `.transcription` and
`.ai_description` made).

### 5.1 The YAML shape — a self-contained `done` record

```yaml
source: decks/Q3.mp4
status: done
engine: vision              # vision | tesseract  (§3.2)
level: fast                 # fast (video frames) | accurate (image)  — §2
generated: 2026-07-16T09:41:00Z
kind: video                 # image | video
language: en-US
stride_seconds: 15          # video only — the sampling stride actually used (§2.2.1)
frames_sampled: 160         # video only — how many frames were read
text: |                     # the flattened, searchable text (the whole point)
  Q3 Revenue — $4.2M
  Creator $29/mo …
blocks:                     # image: positioned observations · video: timecoded entries
  - start: 180.0            # video only: seconds (a RANGE after dedupe, §2.2.3)
    end: 375.0
    text: "Q3 Revenue — $4.2M"
    confidence: 0.97
  # image form:
  # - bbox: [0.12, 0.44, 0.31, 0.06]   # x,y,w,h normalized 0–1
  #   text: "ECONNREFUSED 8787"
  #   confidence: 0.99
```

* **`text`** is the flattened concatenation — it is what search greps and what the tab shows. It is
  **legitimately empty** for a text-free file (§2.3).
* **`blocks[]`** carries the structure: **normalized bounding boxes** for an image (so the viewer can later
  highlight *where* a hit was), **timecoded ranges** for a video (so a hit can **seek**, §1.2).
* A read returns a usable `OcrView` **only** when the artifact exists with `status: done` — the `text` being
  empty does **not** make it null (§2.3, the load-bearing distinction).

### 5.2 The presence indicator

The Storage file table's `analysis` indicator gains **`"ocr"`**, lighting up the moment the `.ocr` artifact

`~/BGit/Bryan_git/LargeFileBridge/code/packages/backend/src/modules/storage/storage-type.service.ts` — `trackingBaseDir(root, type?)` / `usesLfbridgeDir(type)`.
The storage-table indicator that detects the `.ocr` artifact is handled in code:

## 6. Engine readiness — `withOcrReady` (LOCKED)

The third column of §0's readiness row. Transcription gates on a **model download**; description gates on a
**provider key**; **OCR gates on almost nothing** — which is the feature's best property, and the gate exists
only to make the rare failure honest:

* **The common case is ALWAYS ready.** On a Mac, `mac-ocr` is an npm dependency and Vision is in the OS.
  There is **no** setup card, **no** consent dialog, and **no** "Re-check" button in the path the user will
  actually hit. **A ready check that is always true must not cost a dialog.**
* **The two not-ready cases:**
  1. **No engine at all** — the Vision binary won't run (non-Mac, or a broken package) **and** the vendored
     Tesseract fallback is unavailable. Surfaces as `no_engine` with an honest reason.
  2. **A non-vendored language was requested** — the one permissioned download (§3.3), which reuses
     transcription's consent dialog and **never** proceeds silently.
* **Video adds one tool dependency: ffmpeg/ffprobe** (frame extraction, §2.2). When a **video** is OCR'd on a
  machine without ffmpeg, the run **`failed`**s with the install hint (`brew install ffmpeg`) — the same
  honest-outcome posture describe takes for its compress-to-fit ([ai_description.mdx §3.3](./ai_description.mdx)).
  **Images need no external tool at all.** Note the asymmetry this creates and do not paper over it: an
  ffmpeg-less machine OCRs every image fine and every video not at all.
* **Every entry point gates identically — no bypass** (the rule [Transcribe.mdx §5.2.2](./Transcribe.mdx)
  locks): the viewer button, the ⋮ item, the page action, the popup Apply, and the queue worker all route
  through the same gate. There is no path that reaches the engine without it.

### 6.1 The shared launcher (`lib/ocr.ts`) (LOCKED)

The third sibling of `lib/transcribe.ts` / `lib/describe.ts`. **Every** frontend OCR producer calls it, so
the readiness gate, the pending state, the toast, and the query invalidation are written **once**:

`runOcrFile(path)` → flips the control to a disabled **OCR'ing…** spinner **instantly** (the click is never a
no-op — the defect [Transcribe.mdx §2.1](./Transcribe.mdx) fixed), posts, toasts the **honest outcome**, and
invalidates `["ocr"]` + `["progress"]`.

The shared launcher and the readiness gate are handled in code:

## 10. Parallelization (LOCKED)

**The headline: OCR is LOCAL CPU work, so it draws the MASS-COMPUTE core budget** — the opposite of describe.
[parallelization.mdx §3](./parallelization.mdx)'s map gains a row, and getting it right matters because OCR
is the transaction most likely to be run over an entire tree (§1.6).

### 10.1 Which budget, and why (LOCKED)

[ai_description.mdx §12.6](./ai_description.mdx) fans describe **~24-wide** on one premise: *"the machine
sits idle while the provider thinks"* — parallelism is free because the work is a **network round-trip**.
**That premise is false for OCR.** Vision and Tesseract burn **local cores**; nothing waits on a network
(§4). Fanning OCR out 24-wide would be exactly the `cores²` oversubscription
[parallelization.mdx §2](./parallelization.mdx) exists to prevent.

So OCR draws the **mass-compute budget** — `round(cores × max_core_fraction)`, default **90%** — the same
budget compression and batch transcription use. It is *"pure batch CPU work the user explicitly kicked off and
is watching drain in the background"*: the textbook mass-compute consumer.

| Operation | Budget | Fan-out shape | Why |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **OCR — images** | **mass-compute** | **wide**: `budget` jobs, **1 thread each** | One recognition ≈ one core. Textbook single-threaded-tool → fan wide (§2 of parallelization.mdx). |
| **OCR — video frames** | **mass-compute** | **wide over frames**, inside a **core-budgeted** extraction gate (§10.3) | The recognitions are wide; the **ffmpeg extraction** is not (§10.3). |
| **OCR — frame extraction (ffmpeg)** | **mass-compute** | **narrow**: `floor(budget / videoThreads)`, threads **capped** | ffmpeg is internally multi-threaded — the same narrow shape compress-video uses. |

**Anti-oversubscription holds:** `concurrentJobs × threadsPerJob ≈ budget`, never `cores²`.

### 10.2 Two levels of fan-out, one shared limiter

A video is **itself** a batch (160 frames). Naively that is two nested fan-outs — `budget` videos × `budget`
frames = `cores²`, the classic mistake. So the **frame recognitions and the file-level jobs draw from ONE
shared limiter**, exactly as the pin pass does ([pin_process.mdx §4](./pin_process.mdx), the shipped
reference for two-level-one-limiter). A single video being OCR'd alone fans its 160 frames across the whole
budget (fast); 20 videos at once share that same budget between them. **The box is never oversubscribed and
never idle.**

### 10.3 The extraction gate — the describe §12.6.1 lesson, applied UP FRONT (LOCKED)

[ai_description.mdx §12.6.1](./ai_description.mdx) records a defect worth not repeating: a bucket documented
as *"the one bucket where more parallelism is safe even on a small laptop"* turned out to run **up to ~24
concurrent ffmpeg encodes**, because one **conditional CPU branch** (compress-to-fit) hid inside an
"I/O bucket". The rule it locked: **a queue cap may only be called "network-bound" if EVERY path in that job
is network-bound.**

**OCR takes that lesson as a starting condition.** Its video path **always** shells out to ffmpeg — the CPU
branch is not conditional, it is the norm. Therefore:

* **Frame extraction acquires the SHARED transcode slot** — the same semaphore describe's `fitMediaUnderLimit()`
  holds, sized from `coreBudget() / VIDEO_THREADS_PER_ENCODE`. So a describe transcode and an OCR extraction
  **compete for one budget** rather than each believing it owns the machine.
* **Images never touch the gate.** They have no ffmpeg step, so they cost nothing and wait for nothing — the
  common, high-volume case keeps the full wide fan-out.
* **The gate is released before recognition.** The slot covers **only** the ffmpeg pass; the 160 recognitions
  that follow draw the ordinary limiter (§10.2). Holding a transcode slot across recognition would serialize
  the cheap part behind the expensive part's budget.

### 10.4 Async end-to-end — never `spawnSync` (LOCKED)

**The single most important implementation rule in this spec.** Memory: *"'Processing page won't load during
~2000 AI descriptions' = event-loop FREEZE: fit-media.ts used spawnSync on the ~24-way describe hot path."*
That is [performance.mdx P-27](./performance.mdx), theme **T3 — the backend blocks the Node event loop**, and
[ai_description.mdx §3.3.1](./ai_description.mdx) locks it closed.

**OCR must be born with it closed:**

* **Every** child process — `ffprobe`, `ffmpeg` — runs through an **async** runner (`child_process.spawn`,
  awaited, with a hard-kill timeout), **never** `spawnSync`. `ocrOne()` / `extractFrames()` / `probeVideo()`
  are **all `async`** and awaited.
* **Every** file read is `fs.promises` — **never** `readFileSync`. A frame JPEG read on a wide fan-out blocks
  the loop exactly as a base64 payload read did.
* **The Tesseract fallback runs in a WORKER, never on the main thread.** `tesseract.js` recognition is a
  multi-second **CPU-bound WASM** call; run in-process it freezes the loop just as thoroughly as `spawnSync`
  — with none of the visibility, since there is no child process to blame. It uses the library's
  **scheduler + worker pool** ([tesseract.js workers vs schedulers](https://github.com/naptha/tesseract.js/blob/master/docs/workers_vs_schedulers.md)),
  pool size = the core budget, workers **reused** across files (worker startup dominates a small image's
  cost, and *"each worker uses a high amount of memory, so code should never be able to create an arbitrary
  number of workers"*).
* Only the near-instant `which`-style availability probe stays synchronous (a few ms — the same exception
  compression's `onPath()` takes).

**The acceptance test is the sibling's:** fire **2,000 OCR jobs**, then open **`/processing` in a second
browser** — it **loads and moves** within one poll interval ([ai_description.mdx §12.7](./ai_description.mdx)).

### 10.5 The queue bucket

The queue ([job_queue.mdx §3](./job_queue.mdx)) gains an **`ocr`** bucket — or, precisely, **two** caps like
compress's media-aware pair: **`ocr:image`** (wide, mass-compute) and **`ocr:video`** (the extraction gate,
§10.3). `ProgressKind` gains `"ocr"`, so every existing surface — the dock card, the Processing rows, the
Waiting backlog, the per-op counts — works with **no new UI**.

The `ocr` bucket's media-aware caps and the shared extraction gate are implemented in code:
`~/BGit/Bryan_git/LargeFileBridge/code/packages/backend/src/modules/jobqueue/jobqueue.service.ts` — `capFor()` (the `ocr:image` / `ocr:video` buckets, drawn from `coreBudget()`) · `~/BGit/Bryan_git/LargeFileBridge/code/packages/backend/src/modules/describe/fit-media.ts` — `acquireTranscodeSlot()` (the shared semaphore OCR's `extractFrames()` also acquires) · the op union `~/BGit/Bryan_git/LargeFileBridge/code/packages/shared/src/types.ts` — `ProgressKind` (add `"ocr"`).
Policy: [parallelization.mdx §3](./parallelization.mdx).

**OCR ranks last, behind compress.** The rationale is honest and worth stating so nobody "promotes" it later:
an un-pinned file is a **data-loss** risk; an undecided file is an **unmade decision**; a missing transcript
or description is **missing content**; an uncompressed video is **wasted disk**. Missing OCR text is a
**search convenience** — the cheapest of the seven to fix and the least costly to lack. It must never
outrank a backup problem, and on a repo where it is the *only* outstanding item it is, correctly, the whole
recommendation.

The inherited precedence rules still apply: **scan-stale wins** (the primary reads *"Scan now"*),
**all-clear = no button**, and the primary is **gated on click, not on display** — though for OCR that gate
is nearly always a no-op (§6).

`~/BGit/Bryan_git/LargeFileBridge/code/packages/frontend/src/pages/repos/metricWarningDefs.tsx` — `topRecommendation()` (returns `{ metricId, warning, label }`; the `ocr` entry appended **last** in the ordered list), rendered by `~/BGit/Bryan_git/LargeFileBridge/code/packages/frontend/src/pages/repos/OneRepoPage.tsx`.

## 15. Performance — OCR for VIDEO (LOCKED)

The expensive half, and the reason §2's fast level exists.

### 15.1 The cost model

For a clip of duration `D` seconds at stride `S` (default 15):

```
frames        = ⌈D / S⌉
cost          = extract(D)                +  frames × recognize_fast
                └ ONE sequential ffmpeg      └ ~30–60 ms/frame (Vision .fast, 1080p, Apple Silicon)
                  decode+scale pass
```

Worked, on a ~10-core Apple Silicon machine:

| Clip | Frames @15s | Extract | Recognize | **Total** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 5 min | 20 | ~3 s | ~1 s | **~4 s** |
| 40 min (the §1.2 deck) | 160 | ~20 s | ~6 s | **~26 s** |
| 139 min (the longest real file) | 556 | ~70 s | ~22 s | **~90 s** |

**Extraction dominates — by roughly 3×.** That single fact drives every rule below. The naive expectation
("OCR is the slow part") is wrong, and optimizing recognition while ignoring the decode would be optimizing
the cheap half.

### 15.2 The rules (all LOCKED)

1. **One sequential pass, not N seeks.** `-vf fps=1/15` in **one** ffmpeg invocation. `N` separate `-ss`
   seeks pay container-open + keyframe-seek + teardown **per frame** and, on a long clip, cost **multiples**
   of a straight decode. **Never loop `-ss`.**
2. **Downscale in the extraction, not after.** Emit frames at **max 1920px wide** (`-vf fps=1/15,scale=…`)
   — ffmpeg's scaler is far faster than the recognizer's, and text legible at 4K is legible at 1080p. A 4K
   clip that skips this pays ~4× the recognition cost for **zero** accuracy.
3. **`-an` — drop audio.** The audio track is decoded for nothing otherwise. Speech is the transcript's job.
4. **JPEG frames at q≈3, not PNG.** Frames are ephemeral (§2.2) and PNG encoding is a needless CPU + disk
   cost at 160 frames per file.
5. **The extraction holds the shared transcode slot; the recognitions do not** (§10.3).
6. **`fps=1/15` is a filter, not a sample-and-discard.** ffmpeg decodes every frame regardless (it must —
   inter-frame compression), so the win is in **not encoding** 71,999 of 72,000 frames. Do not expect a 15×
   speedup from a 15× stride; expect the encode side to vanish and the decode side to remain. This is why
   §2.2.1 does **not** treat stride as a free performance knob: **halving the stride does not double the
   cost** (the decode is already paid) — it roughly doubles only the recognition third.
7. **Cap the frame count.** A pathological input (a 10-hour stream) would emit 2,400 frames. `max_frames`
   (default **1,000**) bounds it; when it bites, the artifact records `truncated: true` and the UI **says
   so** — a silent cap reads as "we covered it all" when we didn't ([parallelization.mdx](./parallelization.mdx)'s
   no-silent-caps rule).
8. **Delete the temp frames in a `finally`.** 160 JPEGs × 2,000 videos is real disk. They live under the
   state dir, never beside the media.

### 15.3 The 2,000-video scenario

The [use_case_ai_description_at_scale.mdx](./use_case_ai_description_at_scale.mdx) tour, applied: 2,000
videos × ~26 s ≈ **14.4 core-hours**, drained at 90% of 10 cores ≈ **1.6 hours** wall-clock — and, critically,
**with the app fully responsive throughout** (§10.4), every file visible on `/processing`, and the poll payload
bounded ([processing.mdx §5](./processing.mdx)). Compare **describe**'s same-tree run, which was
**provider-rate-limited into an overnight failure cascade** ([ai_description.mdx §3.5](./ai_description.mdx)):
OCR has **no provider**, so it has **no rate limit, no 429s, no timeouts, and no transient-vs-permanent
problem** (§4). Its only ceiling is the box, and the box is ours.

## 16. Performance — OCR for IMAGES (LOCKED)

The cheap, high-volume half — and the one that must never be made slow in the name of the video path.

### 16.1 The cost model

```
cost = decode(image) + recognize_accurate     ≈ 150–400 ms   (Vision .accurate, a 4K screenshot)
```

**No ffmpeg. No frames. No temp files. No gate.** An image is the simplest path in the app: read, recognize,
write. A directory of 1,204 screenshots (§1.1) at 90% of 10 cores ≈ **45 seconds** for the whole folder.

### 16.2 The rules (all LOCKED)

1. **Accurate, always** (§2.1). At ~250 ms there is nothing worth saving — and the `.fast` level's errors are
   precisely the ones that ruin a *search index*, which is the artifact's entire purpose. **Never** apply the
   video path's fast level to a standalone image.
2. **Language correction ON.** It is what turns `rn`→`m` and `0`→`O` confusions into words a user will
   actually search for. On the video path it is **off** (the wrong trade at 160 frames).
3. **Fan WIDE — `budget` jobs, 1 thread each** (§10.1). A single recognition is effectively single-threaded;
   this is the textbook wide fan-out.
4. **Do NOT pre-scale images.** Unlike the video path (§15.2 rule 2), a still is a **one-shot** cost and the
   full resolution is exactly what makes small text (a form label, a stack trace) legible. Rule 2 there and
   this rule here **look contradictory and are not**: one is about 160 frames of redundant pixels, the other
   about the single frame the user actually asked about.
5. **A HEIC is an image.** It routes through the same conversion the media pipeline already uses
   (`magick "file.heic[0]"` — the primary still only; [images.mdx](./images.mdx)), then OCRs normally.
6. **Reuse the Tesseract worker pool across files** (§10.4). At ~250 ms of work, a per-file worker **spawn**
   would dominate the run.
7. **Empty is the common case and must be CHEAP** (§2.3). A text-free photo does a full recognition and
   writes an artifact with `text: ""`. Do not add a "does this look like it has text?" pre-filter to avoid
   it — that heuristic costs about what the recognition costs and would be wrong in the direction that
   matters (skipping a real hit).

## 19. Acceptance criteria

1. **The two speeds (§2):** an **image** OCRs at the **accurate** level with language correction **on**; a
   **video** samples **one frame every 15 s** and OCRs each at the **fast** level with correction **off**. The
   stride is `ocr.video_stride_seconds` (default 15), frames are phased **half a stride** in (never t=0,
   §2.2.2), and consecutive duplicate text collapses into one **time-ranged** entry at ≥95% similarity
   (§2.2.3).
2. **Empty is success (§2.3):** a text-free file yields `status: done`, `text: ""` — the ✓ shows, the status
   icon reads **Done**, the `ocred` metric counts it, and it is **never re-offered**. It is not `failed` and
   not "no artifact".
3. **The engine is a library (§3):** OCR works after `pnpm install` with **no `brew install`**, no Xcode, and
   no user provisioning — `mac-ocr`/Vision primary (its `.fast`/`.accurate` levels **are** the two speeds),
   `tesseract.js` fallback, auto-selected, the resolved engine recorded in the artifact.
4. **Local, offline, forever (§4, §3.3):** OCR makes **zero** network calls. With the network unplugged, both
   engines OCR normally (the Tesseract `traineddata` is **vendored**, `langPath` local). There is no API key,
   no provider matrix, and no credentials popup anywhere in the feature. A non-vendored language is a
   **consented** download, never silent.
5. **Placement (§5):** the artifact is `<trackingBase(root)>/<rel-dir>/<name.ext>.ocr` — extension
   **appended**, path-mirrored, inside the **committed** tracking area, resolved through `trackingBaseDir()`
   (a working repo keeps `.lfbridge/`; an **SDL has none** and an SDL's own `.git/` never demotes it). No
   `*.ocr` git-ignore line is injected. The 3-way placement radios govern it identically to `.transcription`.
6. **A third analysis COLUMN, not a tab (§7):** the viewer's band 6 shows **AI description · Transcription ·
   Text (OCR)** **side by side, all at once** — **no tab strip, no radio buttons, no default-tab rule**
   ([media_viewer.mdx §6](./media_viewer.mdx) is authoritative; ai_description.mdx §1's two-tab description is
   **stale**). **audio** → Transcription only; **image** → AI description + Text; **video** → all three, widths
   dividing evenly among the columns shown. Each ✓ reflects artifact presence **including empty text**. A
   video's OCR rows **seek** on click.
7. **Every entry point exists, with the RIGHT names (§8):** the four producing entry points of
   [dialogs.mdx §6](./dialogs.mdx) — metric tile, header primary, page action-**link**, and ⋮/right-click —
   each carry OCR. The menu item is **Create OCR text** in the **`Create`** group (file **and** directory),
   directly after `Create Transcription(s)` / `Create AI description(s)`. There is **no `Actions ▾` dropdown**
   (page_actions.mdx §0) and **no repo-kebab item** (menus.mdx §5.1 has no producing actions — §8.4). The
   File System (§8.9) and One-Repo (§8.10) inventories are complete and each row resolves as tabulated.
8. **One popup (§9):** every entry point — **including the single-file menu item** — opens **the** unified
   `WarningPopup`-based batch popup, never a native dialog (grep `window.confirm` = 0), always fed by
   `POST /api/ocr/plan` for scope-walking opens, always **stating** the excluded already-done count via
   `excludedClause()` (omitted at 0), with the **"Filter: Videos / Images"** row and the honest per-kind cost
   line, and a solid-blue **"OCR N files"** confirm that is disabled at zero and applies to **visible ∩
   checked**. The **"Opening window…"** spinner appears **synchronously** for scope-walking opens **only** —
   the metric tile and header primary build from in-memory `RepoDetail.files` and take none. **Confirm calls
   `api.ocrEnqueue`** — never `api.ocrBatch`, never inline.
9. **Parallelization (§10):** OCR draws the **mass-compute** budget (**not** describe's ~24 network fan-out).
   Images fan **wide** (budget × 1 thread); video **frame extraction** acquires the **shared transcode slot**
   sized from `coreBudget()`; frames and files draw **one shared limiter** so nesting never reaches `cores²`.
   `concurrentJobs × threadsPerJob ≈ budget`.
10. **Async end-to-end (§10.4):** **no `spawnSync`** and **no `readFileSync`** anywhere on the OCR path; the
    Tesseract fallback runs in a **pooled worker**, never the main thread. Firing **2,000 OCR jobs** and
    opening **`/processing` in a second browser** loads and moves within one poll interval.
11. **Video performance (§15):** **one** ffmpeg pass (`fps=1/15`, never N × `-ss`), downscaled to ≤1920px in
    the extraction, `-an`, JPEG q3, temp frames deleted in a `finally`, `max_frames` (default 1,000) bounding
    pathological inputs and, when it bites, recorded as `truncated: true` and **said out loud**.
12. **Image performance (§16):** no ffmpeg, no temp files, no gate, **no pre-scaling**, accurate level,
    correction on, worker pool reused. ~1,200 screenshots complete in about a minute on a 10-core box.
13. **Symmetry (§0):** the §0 triple table holds — every transcription surface has **both** an AI-description
    and an **OCR** mirror; the module trees `modules/{transcribe,describe,ocr}/`, the launchers
    `lib/{transcribe,describe,ocr}.ts`, the status icons, and the popup launchers
    `open{Transcribe,Describe,Ocr}Batch` are parallel.
14. **Every enum joined (§18.1):** `ProgressKind` + `"ocr"` (which is what makes the dock card, Processing
    rows, Waiting backlog and `queuedByOp` work with **no new UI**); `JobOp`, `Bucket`/`bucketOf`/`capFor`;
    `FileRow.ocr`; `TaskMetrics.{ocrable,ocred}`; `OcrResult`/`OcrBatchResult`/`OcrView`;
    `AppConfigSchema.ocr`; `RepoSettings.ocr.placement`; **`transactions_log.mdx §3.2`'s `op` enum + §6's
    heartbeat per-op counts (`ocr=N`)**; and `storages.mdx §6`'s `analysis: […]` list + `analyzeFile`.
    **§18.1.1:** the To-Do batch enums are a **three-way** gap (describe's kind was never registered either)
    — register `"todo" | "transcribe" | "describe" | "ocr"` **together**, never `ocr` alongside a missing
    `describe`.
15. **Warnings land in BOTH homes (§13):** a new **§10.11 "OCR warnings"** category (`ocr-no-engine`,
    `ocr-video-needs-ffmpeg`, `ocr-failed`, `ocr-language-not-vendored`, `ocr-fallback-engine-in-use`,
    `ocr-frames-truncated`) **and** **§11.1.4 `repo-ocrable-work`** for the metric tile — following
    describe's §10.6 precedent, not transcription's scattered one.
16. **Recommendation ranking (§12.3):** the blue **"OCR ›"** primary ranks **last** of the seven
    (`ipfs-down → pull-down → undecided → transcribe → describe → compress → ocr`) — a search convenience
    never outranks a backup risk, a decision, missing content, or wasted disk. Scan-stale still wins;
    all-clear still shows no button.

### 20.3 The verification that matters (real media, real engine)

Not a mock. Apple Vision, on this machine, through `ocrOne()`:

* **The two-speed rule is EMPIRICALLY the right call.** On a 900×260 image of known text:
  **accurate = 513ms, perfect** (`Creator $29/mo`, confidence 1.000); **fast = 94ms (5.5× faster) but
  CORRUPTED** (`Creator $291mo`, confidence 0.500). That corruption is precisely §16.2 rule 1's argument —
  the fast level's errors are the ones that ruin a *search index* — now measured rather than asserted.
* **A screenshot** (`stacktrace.png`) → `ocred`, 117 chars, read `Error: connect ECONNREFUSED 127.0.0.1:8787`
  correctly (the §1.1 use case).
* **A text-free photo** (`beach.jpg`) → `ocred`, **0 chars**, artifact written with `text: ""`, and the
  **re-run returned `skipped`** — §2.3 and §12.4 holding together, which is the defect that would otherwise
  re-offer every text-free file forever.
* **A 126-second slide deck** with a **black opening frame** → 8 frames at the 15s stride in **846ms**;
  the half-stride phase **dodged the black frame** (§2.2.2); the collapse turned 8 samples into **3
  time-ranged entries** (`0:00–0:45 "Q3 Revenue $4.2M"`, `0:45–1:15 "Creator Plan $29 per month"`,
  `1:15–2:00 "Thank You"`) — §2.2.3 working on real footage.
* **A 13-file tree** (12 images + the video) → `previewOcr` offered 13 with a **9-frame hint** on the video
  row (§9.2); `enqueueOcr` returned the plan **immediately** and the queue drained all 13, peaking at
  **16 of 22 core-slots busy** (12 images × 1 thread + 1 video × 4 threads = 16 ≤ budget) — the
  anti-oversubscription invariant (§10.1) holding in a real run. Re-planning the same tree then offered
  **0 candidates, 13 already done**.

### 20.4 §10.2 caught a real bug in this implementation (worth keeping)

The nested fan-out rule is not theoretical — **the first cut of this module violated it**, and the spec is
what found it. The queue caps *files* (`ocr:video` = `floor(budget / VIDEO_THREADS)` = 5 on a 22-core box),
and the video path then fanned its *frames* out across `coreBudget()` — a fresh 22 **per video**:

```
5 concurrent videos × 22 frames each = 110 recognitions on a 22-core box
```

Exactly the `cores²` over-subscription [parallelization.mdx §2](./parallelization.mdx) calls the load-bearing
invariant. **A per-video `mapLimit(frames, coreBudget())` looks correct in isolation and is wrong in
composition** — a per-call budget cannot see the other calls; only shared state can. That is *why* §10.2 says
"one shared limiter" and not "bound the frames".

Fixed with a module-level semaphore both levels draw from (`withRecognitionSlot()`), the pin pass's
two-level/one-limiter shape ([pin_process.mdx §4](./pin_process.mdx)). **Verified:** 6 videos × 8 frames — 48
recognitions wanting to run at once — peaked at **exactly 22 = the budget**, never above.
